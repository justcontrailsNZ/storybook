title: "The Midas Touch: How Midas Learned to Share"

---

Long ago, after King Midas had already learned that turning everything to gold was a terrible idea, he tried to live more wisely.

But changing a habit can be hard.

Midas still loved riches. He still liked being treated as the most important person in the room. And he still liked to get his own way.

One spring day, a strange visitor arrived near Midas’ palace.

The visitor looked old and tired. His clothes were dusty, and he walked slowly, as if he had travelled a long way.

Some guards laughed. “Go away,” one said. “This is a palace, not a shelter.”

But Midas noticed the man from a distance and felt a small tug in his chest.

“I remember what greed did to me,” Midas thought. “Maybe this is a test.”

Midas walked down the steps and spoke to the visitor.

“Come inside,” Midas said. “You can eat and rest.”

The visitor’s eyes brightened. “Thank you,” he said.

A guest who loves laughter
In many Greek versions of this myth, the visitor was Silenus, a friend and helper of Dionysus, the god of wine and celebration. Silenus loved stories, singing, and jokes.

At the palace, Silenus ate bread and fruit and drank water, then began to tell funny tales.

Servants who usually looked serious started to smile. Even the guards chuckled.

Midas found himself laughing too.

It felt good.

Midas realised something: gold did not make his palace happy. People did.

Dionysus offers a reward
After a few days, Dionysus arrived.

He was not angry. He was pleased that Midas had cared for his friend.

“You treated Silenus kindly,” Dionysus said. “So I will offer you a reward. Ask for something, and I will grant it.”

Midas’ heart jumped. A reward from a god was a huge opportunity.

For a moment, the old greedy thought returned.

“I could ask for more treasure,” Midas thought.

Then he remembered the golden touch—how he couldn’t eat, how he couldn’t drink, how scared he had been.

Midas took a slow breath.

“I want to choose better this time,” he told himself.

So Midas asked for something different.

“I wish,” Midas said to Dionysus, “to be able to know what is enough. I wish to enjoy what I have and to share without feeling scared.”

Dionysus watched him carefully. “That is a difficult wish,” he said. “Are you sure?”

Midas nodded. “Yes.”

Dionysus lifted his hand. “Then your wish is granted,” he said.

But it did not feel like magic lightning or a loud change. It felt like a quiet shift inside Midas’ mind.

A test in the market
Soon after, Midas went into the town market.

He saw a seller with shiny bracelets, a cloth merchant with bright dyed fabric, and a stall filled with sweet cakes.

Midas’ hands twitched. He wanted to buy everything.

But then he paused.

“Do I need this?” he asked himself.

He looked at the cakes and remembered how food matters more than gold. He bought only what he wanted for dinner, not piles of extras.

As he walked, he noticed a family nearby. Their clothes were worn. The parents looked worried. A small child stared at the cakes with hungry eyes.

Midas felt uncomfortable. “I used to look away,” he thought. “Because I didn’t want to share.”

This time, he stopped.

Midas bought extra bread and fruit and offered it to the family.

The parents looked surprised. “Are you sure?” the mother asked.

Midas nodded. “Yes,” he said. “Everyone needs to eat.”

As he walked away, Midas felt something warm inside—not pride, but a calmer feeling.

He had shared, and he still had enough.

A king who changes slowly
Back at the palace, Midas began to change small things.

He made sure workers were paid fairly. He ordered that no one at his gates should be laughed at just because they were poor. He started giving to people in need, not to show off, but because it was the right thing to do.

Sometimes Midas still struggled. Sometimes he still felt that old greedy pull.

But each time, he remembered the lesson: more is not always better.

Why people remember this myth
Greek people told stories about Midas to warn against greed, but also to show that people can learn.

This myth teaches:

It’s possible to make better choices after a mistake.
Sharing can bring real happiness.
Being rich doesn’t matter as much as being kind and fair.
Quick glossary (word help)

greed: wanting too much
enough: as much as you need, not too much
fairly: in a just and equal way
