title: "The Trojan Horse"
---


Long ago, two powerful cities, Troy and the Greek kingdoms, were at war. The city of Troy had tall walls and strong gates. For years, the Greeks tried to break in, but they could not.

The Greek soldiers grew tired. Their camps were dusty. Their armour was heavy. Some wondered if they would ever go home.

A clever Greek leader named Odysseus came up with a new plan.

“We won’t fight the walls,” he said. “We will use a trick.”

Building the horse
The Greeks built a giant wooden horse—taller than a house. It had wheels at the bottom so it could be rolled.

The horse looked like a gift.

Inside the horse, there was a hidden space—big enough for a small group of soldiers to fit. They climbed in quietly and waited, holding their breath.

The rest of the Greek army did something surprising. They packed up their camp, boarded their ships, and sailed away—just far enough to hide.

From the walls of Troy, the Trojans watched the Greek ships disappear.

“They’ve finally given up!” people cheered.

The “gift” arrives
The Trojans found the wooden horse outside their gates. It looked impressive and mysterious.

Some Trojans said, “It’s a gift. Let’s bring it into the city to celebrate!”

But others felt unsure.

One person warned, “Gifts from enemies can be dangerous.”

Still, the excitement was strong. Troy had been under pressure for so long that people wanted to believe the war was over.

They opened the gates and rolled the wooden horse inside the city walls.

That night, the Trojans held a celebration. Music echoed through the streets. People ate and drank and laughed with relief.

No one thought about the horse anymore.

The surprise in the darkness
When the city finally went quiet and most people were asleep, the hidden Greek soldiers climbed out of the horse.

They moved carefully through the shadows. They did not shout. They did not run.

They opened the city gates from the inside.

Outside the walls, the Greek ships had returned under cover of night. The army hurried back into the city.

By morning, Troy’s strong walls did not matter anymore—because the danger had been brought inside.

Why people tell this story
The story of the Trojan Horse is remembered as a warning:

It’s important to think carefully, even when you feel excited.
It’s wise to listen to warnings, even when you want to celebrate.
Sometimes the biggest danger is the one you don’t expect.
Today, when people talk about a “Trojan horse,” they often mean something that looks harmless, but hides a problem.

Quick glossary: enemy (someone you are fighting), strategy (a plan to win), hidden (kept out of sight), warning (advice about danger)
