Long ago, when the world was young and gods walked among mortals, Artemis was the goddess of the moon and the hunt. She was strong, swift, and fiercely independent. She loved the wild outdoors—the quiet of deep forests, the sharp peaks of high mountains, and the endless, star‑lit skies.

One crisp morning, while tracking a deer through a sun‑dappled glade, Artemis met a hunter named Orion. He was tall, with eyes that sparkled like the night, and he moved through the woods with a confidence that matched her own. He too loved the wild places of the earth.

They began to hunt and explore together. They did not always agree—Artemis valued silence and patience, while Orion sometimes rushed ahead—but they respected each other’s abilities. Over time, a deep friendship grew.

Orion would point out animal tracks in the mud, reading the forest floor like a book. Artemis would notice the subtle shifts in the wind and the clouds, predicting the weather before a single drop fell. Sometimes they raced through the trees, laughing as branches whipped past. Sometimes they sat beside a stream, listening to the birds and letting the water cool their tired feet.

<hr style="margin: 2rem 0;">

## 🌲 A Problem Grows

Not everyone was pleased with their bond. Some gods whispered jealously. Others suspected Orion had ambitions beyond friendship. Artemis paid no attention—until Orion’s pride began to show.

In some versions of the myth, Orion started to boast.

“I am the best hunter of all!” he declared one evening, his voice echoing across a clearing. “No beast, no challenge, nothing can beat me!”

Artemis frowned. She admired skill and courage, but she had no patience for arrogance.

“Be careful,” she warned, her tone calm but firm. “Pride can make even the swiftest runner stumble.”

Orion laughed, tossing a pebble into the darkening woods. “I can handle anything,” he said, and the stars above seemed to hold their breath.

<hr style="margin: 2rem 0;">

## ⚡ A Hard Moment

Days later, Orion swam far out into the glittering sea. From the shore, he was barely a speck, a dark shape rising and falling with the waves.

A challenge was whispered among the gods: could Artemis, the perfect archer, hit that distant moving target? Artemis was known to split a falling leaf with her arrow, to pierce a deer’s heart without a sound. Her aim was legendary.

But no one told her the truth—that the speck was Orion.

She drew her bow, felt the familiar tension in the string, and let fly.

The arrow shot across the water, straight and true.

A terrible silence followed.

When Orion did not return to land, Artemis went searching. She found his body floating among the waves, the arrow she had loosed still lodged in his chest. Grief crashed over her like a wave she could not outrun.

<hr style="margin: 2rem 0;">

## ✨ The Stars as a Memory

Artemis was saddened that a moment of confusion and pride could lead to such a loss. She wanted the world to remember Orion not for the mistake, but for the joy he brought to the wild places.

In some versions of the myth, she placed him among the stars as a constellation. Now, when people look up on a clear night, they can see “Orion” shining—his belt, his shoulders, his sword—forever hunting across the sky.

Artemis would often gaze at those stars, remembering the friend who once ran beside her through the woods.

<hr style="margin: 2rem 0;">

## 📖 What the Myth Teaches

This story has been told in many ways across centuries, but it often carries these lessons:

- **Pride can lead to trouble.** Even great talents become fragile when mixed with arrogance.
- **Even the wisest can make mistakes.** Artemis was a goddess, yet she acted without full knowledge—and paid a heavy price.
- **We remember those we love.** When loss happens, people find ways to honour and cherish the memories of those who mattered.
