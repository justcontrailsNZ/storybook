Perseus and Medusa

Long ago, there was a young man named Perseus. He lived quietly with his mum and tried to stay out of trouble.

But a cruel ruler wanted to get rid of Perseus. The ruler pretended to be friendly, then set Perseus a task that sounded impossible.

“I want you to bring me the head of Medusa,” the ruler said.

Perseus felt his stomach drop. Everyone had heard of Medusa.

Medusa was a monster with snakes for hair. People said that if you looked straight into her face, you would turn to stone.

Perseus wanted to refuse. But he also wanted to protect his mum and prove he was not a coward.

“I will do it,” Perseus said, even though his voice shook a little.
Help from the gods
Perseus didn’t know where to begin. Medusa lived far away, and no one wanted to talk about her.

But in Greek myths, the gods often watch what humans do. Some of them decided Perseus deserved help.

The goddess Athena came with a special gift: a shiny shield.

“This shield is polished like a mirror,” Athena told him. “Do not look at Medusa directly. Use the shield to see her reflection.”

Then the god Hermes brought him winged sandals.

“With these,” Hermes said, “you can travel quickly over land and sea.”

Perseus also received a strong bag to carry Medusa’s head safely.

Perseus thanked them. He still felt afraid, but now he had a plan.

The long journey
Perseus travelled for days. He crossed rocky hills and windy beaches. Some nights were cold, and some days the sun was fierce.

As he went, Perseus reminded himself, “Be careful. Be steady. Think first.”

Finally, he reached the place people warned him about: a lonely area of cliffs and caves.

Outside one cave, Perseus saw stone statues scattered around. They looked like they had once been living people.

Perseus’ hands tightened on the shield. “This is real,” he whispered.

Entering Medusa’s cave
The cave was dark inside. Perseus could hear dripping water and the faint hiss of something moving.

He did not walk straight in, staring ahead. Instead, he lifted Athena’s shield and looked into it like a mirror.

In the reflection, he could see shapes in the cave. He moved slowly, one careful step at a time.

Then he saw her.

Medusa was asleep. Her snake hair curled and shifted even while she slept.

Perseus’ heart hammered. This was the moment where one mistake could ruin everything.

He watched the reflection. He kept his eyes on the shield, not on Medusa.

The dangerous moment
Perseus raised his sword. He took a deep breath and acted quickly.

He did what he had come to do—without looking directly at her.

Then, still using the shield to guide him, he placed Medusa’s head into the special bag. He did not peek inside. He did not take chances.

Perseus turned and hurried out of the cave.

Outside, the air felt fresh again. Perseus almost couldn’t believe he had succeeded.

The flight home
Perseus put on the winged sandals and rose into the sky. Below him, the cliffs grew smaller. The sea stretched wide and glittered in the sun.

He flew for a long time, stopping only when he needed rest and water. He kept the bag close and safe.

When Perseus finally returned, the cruel ruler could not believe what he saw.

But Perseus had learned something important on his journey: bravery is not about pretending you aren’t scared. It is about doing the right thing carefully, even when you are scared.

And Perseus used his success to protect his family and stop the ruler from harming anyone else.

Quick glossary (word help)
reflection: an image you see in a mirror or shiny surface
statue: a stone shape of a person or animal
careful: taking time to avoid mistakes or danger
ruler: a leader, like a king
