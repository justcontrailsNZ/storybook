Long ago on a Greek island, there lived a sculptor named Pygmalion. A sculptor is someone who carves stone into shapes, like people or animals.

Pygmalion was very talented. He could carve curls of hair, folds of clothing, and faces that looked almost real.

But Pygmalion also felt disappointed with the people around him. He thought, “Everyone argues. No one listens. No one is kind enough.”

So he decided to live on his own and focus on his work.

A new project
One day Pygmalion began a new sculpture. He chose a smooth piece of ivory (a white material like bone) and started carving carefully.

He carved a face with gentle eyes. He carved hands that looked soft and relaxed. He carved a posture that seemed calm and brave.

As the statue took shape, Pygmalion became quieter. He worked for hours without stopping.

When he finally stepped back, he was surprised.

The statue was the most beautiful thing he had ever made.

It looked so real that Pygmalion almost expected it to breathe.

He named the statue Galatea in some versions of the story.

Feeling lonely
At first, Pygmalion felt proud. Then he felt lonely.

He spoke to the statue as if it could hear him.

“I wish someone like you were real,” he said softly. “Someone gentle and kind.”

He brought the statue gifts—flowers, beads, and ribbons. He kept it clean and safe, as if it mattered like a person.

But when the workshop went quiet at night, the statue stayed a statue.

Pygmalion sighed. “It’s only stone,” he reminded himself. “Only ivory.”

Still, he couldn’t stop hoping.

A festival and a wish
A festival day came for Aphrodite, the goddess of love. People lit fires, sang songs, and brought offerings to her temple.

Pygmalion joined the crowd. He didn’t usually go, but this time he felt he should.

At the temple, Pygmalion stood before Aphrodite’s statue and spoke honestly.

“I don’t want riches,” he said. “I don’t want fame. I only wish for someone in my life who is truly kind.”

He hesitated, embarrassed, then added, “Someone like the statue I made.”

The flames flickered, and Pygmalion felt a strange warmth in the air—like his words had been heard.

Returning home
Pygmalion hurried back to his workshop. The room was quiet, as usual.

The statue stood in the same place.

Pygmalion walked up to it and touched its hand.

He froze.

The hand felt warm.

Pygmalion blinked and touched it again, carefully.

The ivory no longer felt hard and cold. It felt like living skin.

The statue’s fingers moved slightly.

Pygmalion stepped back, shocked. “What is happening?” he whispered.

Then the statue opened its eyes.

A new beginning
In the myth, Aphrodite had granted Pygmalion’s wish. The statue became a real person.

Pygmalion was overwhelmed. He felt joy, but also responsibility.

He understood something important: if you ask for kindness and love, you must be kind and loving too.

Pygmalion promised to treat her with respect. He promised to listen and to care.

The story says they built a life together, not as an artist and a statue, but as two people.

What this myth teaches
Greek people told this story to show:

Creativity can be powerful.
Loneliness can make people wish for connection.
Kindness and respect matter in every relationship.
Quick glossary (word help)
sculptor: an artist who carves stone or wood
ivory: a smooth white material used in old carvings
festival: a special celebration day
respect: treating someone properly and fairly
