title: "Daedalus and Icarus "
---


Long ago, there was a clever inventor named Daedalus. He could build amazing things: tools, puzzles, and machines that seemed like magic.

Daedalus lived on an island with his son, Icarus. But the island’s king did not trust Daedalus. The king would not let Daedalus and Icarus leave.

Days turned into weeks, and weeks turned into months. Icarus grew restless.

“When can we go home?” Icarus asked.

Daedalus looked out at the sea. Ships sailed past, but none would take them.

“We will find a way,” Daedalus promised.

An unusual escape plan
Daedalus thought and thought. The king watched the ports, so they couldn’t escape by ship. Guards watched the roads, so they couldn’t sneak away.

Then Daedalus looked up.

“The king controls the land and sea,” Daedalus said slowly, “but he does not control the sky.”

Daedalus began collecting feathers—small ones and large ones. He gathered wax and strong thread. Each night he worked carefully, shaping feathers into wings.

Icarus watched, amazed. “Are we going to fly?” he asked.

“Yes,” Daedalus said. “But you must listen to me.”

The important warning
When the wings were ready, Daedalus strapped them onto his own arms, then onto Icarus’ arms. The feathers tickled, and the wax smelled warm.

Daedalus held Icarus’ shoulders and looked him in the eyes.

“These wings will carry you,” Daedalus said, “but only if you are sensible. Do not fly too low, or the sea spray will make the feathers wet and heavy. And do not fly too high, or the sun will melt the wax.”

Icarus nodded quickly. “I understand.”

Daedalus wasn’t fully sure Icarus did understand, but there was no time to wait.

Up into the air
They ran into the wind. The wings caught the air, and suddenly their feet lifted from the ground.

Icarus gasped. “We’re flying!”

Below them, the island shrank. The king’s palace looked small. The sea glittered like glass.

Daedalus stayed steady and careful. He watched the waves and checked his wings.

“Stay near me!” he called.

At first, Icarus did.

But flying felt exciting. It felt like freedom. The wind rushed past his ears, and the world looked huge and wonderful.

Icarus began to laugh. “I can go higher!” he shouted.

Too high
“Icarus, no!” Daedalus called. “Remember the sun!”

But Icarus was filled with pride. He rose higher and higher, like a bird racing the clouds.

The air warmed. The sun shone bright overhead.

At first, Icarus didn’t notice anything wrong. Then he felt something drip down his arm.

The wax was melting.

A feather floated away.

Then another.

Icarus’ eyes widened. “Father!” he cried.

He tried to flap his wings harder, but the wings were coming apart.

Feathers scattered into the air like snow.

Icarus fell.

A sad ending and a strong lesson
Daedalus screamed his son’s name and circled down, but he could not catch him in time. Daedalus was heartbroken.

He kept flying until he reached safety, because he had to survive. But his heart was heavy.

Daedalus never forgot what happened. And people who heard the story understood the lesson too:

Rules are not there to spoil your fun. Sometimes, rules are there to protect you—especially when you feel excited and brave.

Quick glossary (word help)
inventor: someone who makes new things
wax: a soft material that melts when hot
sensible: making safe, careful choices
freedom: being able to go where you want
