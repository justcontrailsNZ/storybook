title: "King Midas and the Golden Touch"

Long ago, there was a king named Midas. He lived in a large palace with tall columns and shining floors. He wore rich clothes and had servants to bring him anything he wanted.

But Midas always wanted more.

He loved counting his treasures. He loved the sound of coins clinking. He loved gold more than almost anything.

One day, Midas helped a visitor who was lost and tired. Midas gave him food and a place to rest. The visitor was grateful and offered Midas a reward.

“You have been kind,” the visitor said. “Ask for something, and I will grant it.”

Midas did not stop to think. He only thought of gold.

“I wish,” Midas said, “that everything I touch turns to gold!”

The visitor looked serious. “Are you sure?”

Midas nodded. “Yes!”

And the wish was granted.

A wish that feels amazing… at first
At first, King Midas was thrilled. He reached out and touched a wooden chair.

Instantly, it became solid gold.

He laughed with joy. He tapped a table—gold! He touched a vase—gold! He ran through his palace, turning ordinary things into treasure.

“My palace will be the richest in the world!” he shouted.

His servants stared. Some looked impressed. Others looked worried.

Midas didn’t notice. He was too excited.

The problem appears
Soon it was time for lunch. Midas sat at his table, still smiling.

A servant brought fresh bread, fruit, and water.

Midas reached for the bread.

It turned into gold.

He tried a grape.

Gold.

He picked up his cup to drink.

Gold.

Midas froze. His smile vanished.

He tried again, more gently, as if being careful could fix it. But every time his fingers touched food or drink, it changed.

Midas’ stomach growled. His mouth felt dry.

“This can’t be right,” he muttered. “How will I eat? How will I drink?”

For the first time, Midas felt afraid of his own wish.

Not everything should be gold
Midas walked around his palace. He looked at golden chairs and golden decorations, but they didn’t make him feel happy anymore.

They felt heavy and useless.

He thought about the simple things he had never noticed before: warm bread, cool water, a soft blanket, a friendly hand.

Midas realised his wish had taken away something important.

He ran outside and called for help. “Please!” he begged. “Take this power away. I was foolish!”

The visitor returned and listened. “You have learned,” the visitor said. “Now you must undo the wish.”

Washing away the golden touch
Midas was told to go to a nearby river and wash his hands in the flowing water.

Midas hurried there as fast as he could. He knelt at the riverbank and dipped his hands in.

He scrubbed and scrubbed.

The water ran over his fingers, cold and clear. It felt like a fresh start.

Then Midas picked up a small stone from the river.

It stayed a stone.

Midas picked up a leaf.

It stayed a leaf.

Midas let out a long breath he didn’t know he was holding.

“It’s gone,” he said, relieved.

A changed king
King Midas returned to his palace. The rooms still held many treasures, but now he understood something he had ignored.

Gold is valuable, but it is not what keeps you alive.

From that day on, Midas tried to be a better king. He still liked nice things, but he stopped letting greed control him.

And he never forgot the lesson of the golden touch: wanting more and more can ruin the good things you already have.

Quick glossary (word help)
treasure: valuable things like gold and jewels
wish: something you want to happen
greed: wanting too much, especially money or power
valuable: worth a lot
