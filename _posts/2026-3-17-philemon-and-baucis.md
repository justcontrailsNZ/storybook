title: "Philemon and Baucis"

Long ago in Ancient Greece, there was a small village surrounded by fields and trees. Most people there were busy and careful with what they had. They did not like strangers, because strangers might ask for food or a place to stay.

But on the edge of the village lived an elderly couple named Philemon and Baucis.

They were poor, and their house was small. Their roof leaked when it rained, and their chairs were worn from years of use. But Philemon and Baucis were known for one thing: they were always kind.

“One day,” Baucis would say, “someone might need help. And we would hope someone helps us too.”

Two strangers arrive
One evening, two travellers walked into the village. Their cloaks were dusty and their feet were sore.

They knocked on one door, then another.

“Please,” they asked, “can we have a little food and a place to rest for the night?”

But the villagers shook their heads.

“No,” said one person, closing the door.

“We have nothing to spare,” said another.

The travellers kept walking. The sky was turning dark, and the air was getting cold.

At last, they reached the tiny house of Philemon and Baucis. It was not grand, but there was warm light in the window.

Philemon opened the door.

“Good evening,” one traveller said politely. “We have nowhere to go.”

Philemon didn’t hesitate. “Come in,” he said. “You can rest here.”

Baucis hurried over with a smile. “Please, sit. We don’t have much, but we will share.”

A simple meal, given gladly
Philemon and Baucis worked together to make what they could.

Baucis brought bread, olives, and a small bowl of beans. Philemon poured water and warmed it near the fire.

They apologised again and again. “It’s not fancy,” Baucis said.

The travellers looked grateful. “Kindness is more important than fancy,” one replied.

While they ate, Philemon and Baucis noticed something strange. Each time Baucis refilled the cup, it seemed to fill itself again. The bowl never seemed to empty.

Philemon’s eyes widened. He leaned close to Baucis and whispered, “This is not ordinary.”

Baucis nodded, nervous now. “Who are they?”

The truth is revealed
When the meal was finished, the travellers stood up. Their tired faces seemed to change. They looked taller and brighter, and the room felt suddenly still.

“We are not ordinary travellers,” one said.

Philemon and Baucis held their breath.

In the myth, the strangers were Zeus (king of the gods) and Hermes (the messenger god). They had come in disguise to see how people would treat strangers.

Hermes spoke calmly. “Most of the village turned us away.”

Zeus looked at Philemon and Baucis. “But you welcomed us. You shared what little you had.”

Philemon and Baucis felt shocked. Then they bowed, trembling.

“We didn’t know,” Philemon said. “We only did what felt right.”

A difficult moment for the village
Zeus led Philemon and Baucis outside.

“Follow us,” Zeus said. “Do not look back.”

The couple followed up the hill behind their home. When they reached the top, Zeus told them to stop and look down.

The village below was covered by rising water. (In this myth, it was a punishment for unkindness.) The fields and streets disappeared beneath a flood.

Philemon and Baucis felt sad, even though the villagers had been selfish.

Baucis whispered, “I wish they had chosen kindness.”

A surprising reward
Then Zeus pointed to the place where Philemon and Baucis’ small house had been.

It was no longer a tiny home.

In its place stood a beautiful temple, strong and safe, with shining steps and tall pillars.

Philemon gasped. “Is that… our home?”

“It is now,” Zeus said. “And you may make a wish.”

Philemon and Baucis looked at each other. They did not wish for gold or power.

Baucis spoke first. “We wish to serve here together,” she said, “and to never be parted.”

Zeus nodded. “Your wish is granted.”

So Philemon and Baucis became caretakers of the temple. They welcomed travellers, offered water and food when they could, and treated people with respect.

The end of the story
Many years later, when they were very old, Philemon and Baucis stood outside the temple and looked at the peaceful land.

They had lived the way they believed was right.

In the myth, they were changed into two trees that grew side by side, their branches gently touching. It was a symbol that they stayed together, just as they wished.

What this myth teaches
Greek people told this story to remind themselves of hospitality—being kind to guests and strangers.

It teaches:

You don’t have to be rich to be generous.
Kindness is a choice, even when life is hard.
How you treat others matters.
