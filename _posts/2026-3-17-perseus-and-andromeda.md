title: "Perseus and Andromeda"
---


Long ago, after Perseus had finished a dangerous journey, he travelled along the coast with the wind in his face and the sea below him.

Perseus was tired, but he kept moving. He wanted to get home safely.

As he flew, he noticed something strange on the shore.

On a rocky cliff near the waves, a girl was chained to a stone.

Perseus circled lower, shocked.

He landed carefully and approached.

“Are you all right?” he asked.

The girl looked frightened but brave. “My name is Andromeda,” she said. “No, I’m not all right. I’m being left here.”

“Why?” Perseus demanded.

Andromeda swallowed. “My mother bragged that I was more beautiful than the sea nymphs,” she explained. “The sea god became angry. A sea monster has been sent to punish our land. My parents were told the monster would leave only if I was given to it.”

Perseus stared at the crashing waves.

“That’s not fair,” he said.

Andromeda’s voice shook. “I’m scared,” she admitted. “But I don’t want my people to suffer.”

Perseus looked at the chains. Then he looked out at the sea again.

“I won’t let this happen,” Perseus said.

The monster arrives
The sea grew darker and rougher. A deep sound rumbled under the water, like a giant moving below the surface.

Then it appeared.

A huge sea monster rose up, pushing waves aside. It opened its mouth and roared.

Perseus’ heart pounded, but he stayed focused.

He had faced danger before. He also had something important: the special bag holding Medusa’s head, which could turn living things to stone if they looked at it.

Perseus did not want to use it unless he had to. But he knew this was an emergency.

He said to Andromeda, “Close your eyes and look away. No matter what happens, don’t look at me.”

Andromeda nodded quickly and turned her head.

Perseus took a steady breath and stepped toward the edge of the cliff.

Perseus’ plan
As the monster came closer, Perseus used speed and careful movement. In some stories he flew above it, staying out of reach. In others he dodged and climbed over rocks.

The monster snapped its jaws, trying to reach him.

Perseus realised he needed to end this quickly before Andromeda was hurt.

He reached into the bag.

He did not look directly at Medusa’s face. He was careful, just as he had been in the cave long ago.

He turned the bag so the monster would see.

The monster froze.

Its skin hardened.

In seconds, it became a stone statue in the sea, like a dark rock sticking out of the water.

The waves continued to crash around it, but it could not move.

Perseus exhaled. “It’s over,” he said.

Freeing Andromeda
Perseus hurried to Andromeda and began to undo the chains.

As soon as she was free, Andromeda stepped away from the cliff edge, her knees trembling.

“You saved me,” she said quietly.

Perseus nodded. “You shouldn’t have been punished for someone else’s bragging,” he said.

Together they walked back toward the city.

A new choice
When Andromeda’s parents and people saw her alive, they were amazed. Some cried with relief.

They thanked Perseus again and again.

Perseus did not ask for treasure. He did not boast. He simply said, “Treat people fairly. Don’t let pride cause harm.”

Andromeda looked at Perseus and said, “I want to choose what happens next in my life.”

Perseus respected her words.

In many versions of the myth, Perseus and Andromeda later travel together and start a new life.

Why people remember this myth
Greek people told this story to show that a hero is not only someone who fights. A hero is also someone who protects people who are in danger and who stands up for fairness.

It also teaches:

Bragging can cause real problems.
Courage often means helping someone weaker than you.
Respecting other people’s choices matters.
