title: "Phaethon and the Chariot of the Sun"

Long ago, a boy named Phaethon wanted to prove something important. He wanted everyone to believe he was the son of Helios, the god who drove the sun across the sky each day.

Some children teased Phaethon.

“If Helios is your father,” they said, “prove it!”

Phaethon felt upset. He decided to visit Helios and ask for help.

A promise from Helios
Phaethon travelled until he reached Helios’ shining palace. Everything looked bright—walls like gold, floors like polished stone.

Helios greeted him warmly. “My child,” he said, “I am proud of you. Ask for a gift, and I will grant it.”

Phaethon thought, “This is my chance.”

“I want to drive your sun chariot for one day,” he said.

Helios’ smile vanished. “No,” he said quickly. “That is too dangerous. Even I find it hard. The horses are powerful, and the sky road is steep.”

But Helios had already promised. In Greek myths, a god’s promise is serious.

Helios tried again. “Choose something else—anything!”

Phaethon shook his head. “I want this.”

The sky journey begins
The next morning, Phaethon climbed into the sun chariot. The horses stamped and snorted, impatient to run.

Helios gave him final advice.

“Hold the reins tightly,” Helios said. “Stay on the middle path. Don’t go too high or too low. And if you get scared, do not panic.”

Phaethon nodded, but his hands were shaking.

“Go,” Helios whispered.

The horses leapt forward.

At first, Phaethon felt thrilled. He was racing through the sky! The world below looked small and quiet.

“I’m doing it!” he shouted.

Losing control
But the horses could tell something was different. They knew Helios was not holding the reins.

They began to move wildly.

The chariot climbed too high, and the air grew cold and thin. Then it plunged too low, and the heat scorched the land.

Rivers dried. Plants wilted. People covered their faces from the burning sun.

Phaethon pulled the reins, but the horses were too strong. He didn’t know the sky road the way Helios did.

“Stop!” Phaethon cried. “Please!”

The horses did not listen.

A hard rescue
The gods could see the world was in trouble. Something had to be done to stop the runaway chariot.

A powerful lightning bolt struck near the chariot, forcing it to stop. Phaethon fell from the chariot and landed far below, into a river, where the water cooled him.

Helios rushed to find him. Helios was filled with sadness.

“I warned you,” Helios said softly. “Not because I didn’t trust you, but because I wanted to keep you safe.”

Phaethon realised his mistake. He had wanted to prove himself so badly that he had ignored good advice.

Why people remember it
Greek people told this myth to explain why some places are very hot and dry, and why the sun must follow a steady path.

It also teaches a clear lesson: some jobs are dangerous, and it is wise to practise and listen before taking on something too big.

Quick glossary: reins (straps to control horses), chariot (a cart pulled by horses), panic (sudden fear), advice (helpful guidance)
