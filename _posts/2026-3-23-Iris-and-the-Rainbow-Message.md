title: "Iris and the Rainbow Message"
---

Long ago, the Greek people told stories about how the gods sent messages across the world. The gods did not use phones or letters. They used messengers.

One messenger was Hermes, who could travel fast.

Another messenger was Iris.

Iris was connected to rainbows. In Greek myths, a rainbow was like a bright road in the sky that Iris could travel along.

A problem on Earth
One year, a long dry season arrived. The land became dusty and cracked. Streams shrank. Farmers worried about their crops.

In one town, people gathered near a dry riverbed.

“If it doesn’t rain soon,” a farmer said, “we won’t have enough food.”

A child looked up at the hard blue sky. “Did the gods forget us?” she asked.

The town’s leader decided to pray. People stood together and asked for help, speaking into the hot air.

Up on Olympus, the gods heard the prayers. Some argued about what to do.

“It’s not our problem,” one god said.

“It is their problem,” said another.

But Hera, who often sent Iris on errands, decided a message must be delivered quickly.

“Iris,” Hera called, “go to the cloud spirits and tell them to bring rain to the dry land. And go fast.”

Iris bowed. “At once.”

Iris travels the sky
Iris stepped onto the edge of Olympus and looked down at the world. She could see distant mountains, the sea, and the dry brown land where people waited.

She lifted her arm, and a rainbow appeared—bright colours stretching across the sky.

Iris ran along the rainbow like it was a bridge. The air rushed past her. The world below looked tiny.

As she travelled, she saw other places too: forests that were still green, lakes that sparkled, and towns that looked like dots.

But she kept going toward the dry land, because that was where the message mattered.

Finding the cloud spirits
Iris reached the place where clouds gathered—high and cold, where the air felt thin. In the myth, cloud spirits moved water through the sky like careful workers.

They were busy, but they did not seem to notice the dry town far below.

Iris called out, “Cloud spirits! Hera sends a message!”

The cloud spirits turned, surprised.

Iris spoke clearly. “The people below need rain. Their river is dry. Their crops are failing. Bring water to their fields.”

One cloud spirit frowned. “We have many places to cover,” it said. “Why should we hurry to one small town?”

Iris did not argue angrily. She spoke firmly.

“Because they are trying. They are working and sharing. They asked for help, and help has been requested.”

The cloud spirits looked at each other. They nodded.

“Very well,” they said. “We will go.”

Rain and a sign of hope
The clouds gathered and thickened. Wind rose over the dry land. People looked up, shielding their eyes.

A low rumble rolled across the sky.

Then rain began to fall—first in light drops, then in a steady shower.

Children laughed and danced. Farmers lifted their faces and sighed with relief. The dusty ground darkened and softened.

As the storm passed, sunlight broke through the clouds.

A rainbow appeared over the town.

The people pointed. “Look!” they shouted. “A sign!”

In this myth, the rainbow reminded them that a message had been delivered—and that hope could arrive even after a hard season.

What this myth teaches
Greek people used stories like this to explain rainbows after storms.

It also teaches:

Asking for help is okay.
Messengers and communication matter.
After difficult times, things can improve.

spirits: magical beings in myths
