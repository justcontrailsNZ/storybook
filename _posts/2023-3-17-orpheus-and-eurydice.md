title: "Orpheus and Eurydice"
---

Long ago, there was a musician named Orpheus. He played an instrument called a lyre, and his music was so beautiful that people would stop walking just to listen.

When Orpheus played, angry voices calmed down. Sad people felt comfort. Even animals would sit quietly nearby.

Orpheus loved his wife, Eurydice, very much. They were happy together.

But one day, Eurydice died, and Orpheus felt as if the world had gone dark.

He couldn’t eat. He couldn’t sleep. He wandered, playing sad songs that made others cry too.

Then Orpheus made a brave decision.

“I will go to the Underworld,” he said, “and I will ask for Eurydice to return.”

The journey to the Underworld
The Underworld was the land of the dead in Greek myths. People believed it was deep below the ground, far from sunshine.

Orpheus travelled until he reached a place where the air felt cold and still. He stepped into dark tunnels and followed them down.

At the entrance, guards tried to stop him. Some stories say a fierce dog guarded the gate.

Orpheus didn’t fight. He didn’t shout.

He played his lyre.

The music floated through the darkness like a soft light. The guards paused. Their angry faces relaxed.

And they let Orpheus pass.

Speaking to the rulers
Orpheus walked deeper until he reached the rulers of the Underworld. They sat quietly, listening to the silence of their kingdom.

Orpheus lifted his lyre and played again.

His song sounded like love, memories, and hope. It sounded like the way sunlight feels after rain.

Even the rulers were moved.

“At last,” they said, “someone who speaks from the heart.”

Orpheus bowed. “Please,” he begged. “Let Eurydice return with me.”

The rule
The rulers agreed—but only on one condition.

“Eurydice may follow you,” they said, “but you must walk in front of her all the way back to the living world. You must not turn around to look at her until you both reach the sunlight. If you look back too soon, she must return here forever.”

Orpheus swallowed.

It sounded simple. But Orpheus knew it would be hard.

Still, he agreed. “I promise.”

The long walk up
Orpheus began walking up through the tunnels. Behind him, he could hear footsteps.

Tap. Tap. Tap.

Eurydice was there.

Orpheus wanted to see her. He wanted to know she was safe. He wanted to reach back and hold her hand.

But he kept walking.

The tunnel seemed endless. The air grew slightly warmer. Orpheus could see a faint glow ahead.

Then doubt crept into his mind.

“What if I imagined those footsteps?” he worried. “What if she isn’t there? What if the rulers tricked me?”

The glow grew brighter. Orpheus was almost at the end.

Just before the sunlight, Orpheus couldn’t stand it any longer.

He turned his head.

For one brief moment, he saw Eurydice behind him—close enough to touch.

But she looked sad.

And then she faded away like mist.

What Orpheus learned
Orpheus fell to his knees, heartbroken.

He had been so close.

This myth teaches that trust can be difficult, especially when you feel afraid. It also shows how worry can change our choices at the worst time.

Orpheus’ music was powerful, but even he could not undo a choice made in panic.
