# 📚🌿 Tāne and the Three Baskets of Knowledge 🌿📚

In the beginning, people knew very little. They did not understand the stars, the seasons, or how to grow food. The god **Tāne** saw that his children—the humans—needed wisdom to live well.

Tāne decided to climb to the highest heaven, where the supreme god Io lived, and ask for knowledge.

---

## 🧗 A Long Climb

The path to the highest heaven was not easy. Tāne had to climb through twelve layers of the sky. Each layer had guardians who tested his courage.

Tāne did not give up. He climbed through storms, darkness, and silence. Finally, he reached the twelfth heaven, where Io waited.

Io was pleased with Tāne’s determination. “You have come far,” Io said. “What do you seek?”

“I seek knowledge for my people,” Tāne replied.

---

## 🧺 Three Baskets

Io gave Tāne three baskets, each made of woven flax.

The first basket was called **Te Kete Tuauri** (the basket of sacred knowledge). It contained wisdom about rituals and prayers.

The second basket was **Te Kete Tuātea** (the basket of worldly knowledge). It held understanding of nature, farming, and the stars.

The third basket was **Te Kete Aronui** (the basket of things we strive for). It held knowledge of art, peace, and kindness.

“These are for your people,” Io said. “But they must be shared with care.”

---

## 🏔️ Returning with Wisdom

Tāne carried the baskets back down through the heavens. When he reached the earth, he called all the people together. He taught them the sacred chants, how to plant crops, how to read the sky, and how to live in harmony.

From that day, knowledge spread. People built schools (whare wānanga) and passed down the teachings. They never forgot that wisdom was a gift to be cherished.

---

## 💡 What This Story Teaches

This story reminds us:

> Knowledge is precious and should be shared.  
> Learning takes effort and courage.  
> Wisdom is not just for one person—it is for the whole community.
