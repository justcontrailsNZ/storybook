Theseus and the Minotaur
Long ago in Ancient Greece, the city of Athens had a serious problem. Every few years, Athens had to send a group of young people across the sea to the island of Crete. It was a sad payment to the powerful King Minos of Crete.

No one in Athens wanted to go.

That was because on Crete there was a huge stone maze called the Labyrinth. It had long twisting paths, dead ends, and dark corners. People said that once you entered, you could not find your way out again.

And inside the Labyrinth lived a terrifying creature called the Minotaur—half man and half bull.

A brave prince of Athens named Theseus could not stand the idea of more young people being taken away.

“This isn’t fair,” Theseus said. “I will go to Crete. I will face the Minotaur, and I will end this.”

Some people tried to talk him out of it. “It’s too dangerous,” they warned. “No one escapes the Labyrinth.”

But Theseus did not change his mind.

Sailing to Crete
Theseus joined the group boarding the ship. The sea was choppy, and the wind snapped the sail. The young people sat quietly, worried and pale.

Theseus tried to sound calm. “Stay close to each other,” he said. “Whatever happens, we will be brave.”

When they arrived in Crete, tall guards led them to the palace. The palace was grand, but it felt cold and unfriendly. Theseus was taken to stand before King Minos.

The king looked at Theseus with interest. “You look strong,” he said. “Are you here to be a hero?”

“Theseus is my name,” he replied. “And I am here to stop the Minotaur.”

King Minos laughed. “Many have tried.”

Ariadne’s plan
That night, as Theseus sat thinking, someone quietly approached. It was Ariadne, the king’s daughter.

“I know who you are,” Ariadne whispered. “And I know what my father does is wrong. The Labyrinth is dangerous, but there may be a way.”

Theseus leaned forward. “Tell me.”

Ariadne held out two things: a sharp sword, and a small ball of string.

“If you go in without a plan, you will get lost,” she said. “Tie one end of the string at the entrance. As you walk, unwind it behind you. After you find the Minotaur, follow the string back out.”

Theseus stared at the ball of string. It seemed so ordinary. But he understood at once: it was a clever idea.

“Thank you,” Theseus said softly. “I will not waste your help.”

Into the Labyrinth
The next day, guards marched Theseus and the others to the Labyrinth. The stone walls rose high, like a fortress. The entrance looked like a dark mouth.

Theseus’ heart pounded, but he kept his face steady.

He tied the string tightly to a stone near the entrance. Then he stepped inside.

The air felt cool and dusty. The Labyrinth was silent at first. Theseus walked slowly, unwinding the string as he went. Left turn. Right turn. Another turn. Some paths led nowhere, so he had to turn back.

After a while, he heard a sound—heavy breathing, like a huge animal nearby.

Then he heard a low growl.

The Minotaur appeared in the shadows.

It was enormous. Its horns curved above its head. Its eyes were wild. The Minotaur stamped the ground and snorted.

For one moment, Theseus wanted to run. But he remembered the young people behind him, and he remembered Athens.

He raised his sword. “No more,” he said.

The fight
The Minotaur charged.

Theseus jumped aside. The stone floor shook. The Minotaur swung its head, trying to hit Theseus with its horns.

Theseus moved carefully. He watched. He waited for the right moment.

When the Minotaur rushed again, Theseus used his speed and strength. The fight was hard and frightening, but Theseus kept going.

At last, the Minotaur fell.

Theseus stood panting, holding his sword. He had done it.

Finding the way out
Now came the next challenge: escaping.

Theseus looked around at the twisting tunnels. Without a guide, he could have been trapped forever.

Then he saw the string on the ground, stretching away behind him like a path.

“Theseus!” one of the young people whispered. “Can we leave now?”

“Yes,” he said. “Follow me.”

They hurried through the tunnels, staying close together. Theseus held the string and followed it back, turn by turn, until they reached the entrance.

The sunlight outside felt bright and warm.

Home again
That night, Theseus and the young people ran to their ship and sailed away from Crete as fast as they could.

When they arrived back in Athens, families cried with relief. The city celebrated.

Theseus had not only been brave—he had also been smart enough to accept help and use a plan.

And people remembered this lesson: courage is important, but courage with a good idea is even stronger.
