title: "Deucalion and Pyrrha: The Great Flood"

Long ago, Greek people told a story about a time when the world became noisy, unkind, and unfair. People argued, cheated, and hurt one another. They stopped caring about what was right.

The gods watched from above.

Some gods were saddened. Others were angry.

Zeus, the king of the gods, decided the world needed a fresh start.

He planned a great flood to wash everything away.

But not everyone was bad. There were still some good people.

Among them were Deucalion and his wife Pyrrha. They tried to live kindly and honestly, even when others did not.

A helpful god warned them, “A flood is coming. Build something that can float. Take food and water. Be ready.”

Deucalion listened. Pyrrha listened too.

They worked quickly and built a strong wooden chest, like a simple boat with walls. They packed what they could carry: dried food, water, and warm blankets.

Then the rain began.

The flood arrives
At first, it was just heavy rain. Then it became a storm that did not stop.

Rivers rose and spilled over their banks. Streams became rushing water. The sea pushed farther onto the land.

Deucalion and Pyrrha climbed into their chest and closed the lid as best they could.

Soon the water lifted them.

They floated, not knowing where they were going. The chest bumped and rocked as waves moved it along.

Inside, Pyrrha held Deucalion’s hand.

“I’m scared,” she whispered.

“So am I,” Deucalion said. “But we’re together.”

Days passed. The rain continued. Everything outside was water and grey sky.

They could not see towns or fields anymore. The world felt empty.

A quiet world
At last, the rain slowed. The sky began to clear.

The chest floated gently until it bumped against land.

Deucalion opened the lid.

They were near a mountain. The ground was muddy and silent. No voices. No animals. No signs of people.

Pyrrha looked around and felt tears in her eyes.

“We’re alone,” she said.

Deucalion’s throat felt tight. “We survived,” he said softly. “But what now?”

They walked carefully, calling out, but no one answered.

Asking for guidance
Deucalion and Pyrrha found a temple and asked the gods what to do.

“We want the world to live again,” they said. “Please tell us how.”

An answer came as a riddle:

“Cover your heads and throw the bones of your mother behind you.”

Pyrrha stepped back, shocked. “That sounds horrible!” she said. “We can’t do that.”

Deucalion thought hard. “It must mean something else,” he said.

Then he understood.

“Our mother could mean Mother Earth,” he said. “And her bones could mean stones.”

Pyrrha stared at the rocky ground. “Stones,” she repeated.

They decided to try.

A new beginning
Deucalion and Pyrrha covered their heads with their cloaks. Then they picked up stones and threw them behind their shoulders.

Something strange happened.

The stones Deucalion threw began to change shape. They became people—strong and steady.

The stones Pyrrha threw also changed. They became people too.

Soon, there were more and more humans, stepping carefully onto the muddy land, blinking at the sunlight.

The world was not empty anymore.

Deucalion and Pyrrha felt relief.

They had been given a new chance—not just to live, but to build a kinder world.

Why people remember this myth
Greek people told this story to explain storms and floods, and to remind themselves of hope after disaster.

It also teaches that:

Even when things go wrong, a new start is possible.
Listening to warnings and planning ahead can save lives.
Kindness matters, especially when the world feels difficult.
Quick glossary (word help)
flood: when water covers land that is usually dry
warn: to tell someone danger is coming
temple: a building for worship
riddle: a puzzling message with a hidden meaning
