title: "Hermes and the First Lyre"
---


Long ago, on a quiet morning, a baby god named Hermes was born. Even as a baby, Hermes was clever and curious.

While other babies slept and cried, Hermes watched, listened, and planned.

On the very first day of his life, Hermes decided he wanted an adventure.

A surprising discovery
Hermes crawled out of his cradle and wandered outside. He found a small tortoise slowly walking through the grass.

Hermes picked it up gently. “Hello,” he said. “You look like you’re carrying a little house.”

Then Hermes had an unusual idea.

He used the tortoise shell to create something new. He stretched strings across it and made the first lyre (a small string instrument).

When Hermes plucked the strings, the sound was clear and sweet.

Hermes smiled. “Music,” he whispered. “I made music.”

Trouble with Apollo’s cattle
But Hermes did not stop there. He also wanted to prove he could be tricky.

Not far away, Apollo had a herd of fine cattle. Hermes decided to take them as a prank.

He moved them quietly and even tried to hide their tracks by making them walk in a confusing way.

Hermes thought, “No one will know it was me.”

But Apollo was not easy to fool. He noticed his cattle were missing and soon discovered Hermes was involved.

Apollo marched to Hermes’ home, angry and ready to argue.

Hermes lay in his cradle, pretending to be an innocent baby.

Apollo pointed. “That baby stole my cattle!”

Hermes blinked wide eyes. “Me? I’m only a baby,” he said, trying to sound confused.

Apollo crossed his arms. “Don’t pretend.”

A trade instead of a fight
The gods brought Hermes and Apollo together to sort out the problem. Hermes knew he was in trouble.

So Hermes made a smart choice. He picked up his new instrument and began to play.

The music was so beautiful that Apollo’s anger softened. He listened closely.

“I’ve never heard anything like that,” Apollo admitted.

Hermes stopped playing. “I made it,” he said. “It’s called a lyre.”

Apollo looked thoughtful. He cared about music, and the lyre was special.

Hermes said, “I will return your cattle. In return, you take the lyre.”

Apollo agreed.

Hermes returned the cattle, and Apollo received the lyre. From then on, Apollo became strongly linked with music and the lyre in Greek stories.

What this myth teaches
This myth shows Hermes as clever and creative—but also a bit cheeky.

It also teaches an important idea: when you make a mistake, it’s better to fix it and make peace than to keep arguing.

Quick glossary: tortoise (a slow animal with a shell), lyre (a string instrument), prank (a trick), trade (swap one thing for another)
