# 🛶🌊 The Story of the Kurahaupō Waka 🌊🛶

The waka **Kurahaupō** was one of the great migration canoes. It was captained by **Ruatea**, a chief from Hawaiki. The name “Kurahaupō” means “reddish night” or “red clouds of night”, a name that spoke of the journey’s challenges.

On the voyage, a fierce storm struck. The canoe was damaged, and many feared it would sink. But Ruatea prayed to the gods, and the storm passed. They sighted land at **Te Tai Tokerau**—the Northland coast.

---

## 🏝️ A New Beginning

The Kurahaupō landed at Rangaunu Harbour. The crew dispersed across the land, establishing settlements and forming new tribes. Ruatea’s descendants became the Ngāti Kahu, Ngāpuhi, and other northern iwi.

The waka was eventually laid to rest, but its legacy lived on. The people kept the stories alive, teaching their children about the courage of Ruatea and the ancestors who braved the ocean.

---

## 🌟 The Legacy

The Kurahaupō is remembered as a waka of resilience. It survived storms and carried the seeds of many northern tribes. To this day, its descendants honour it with song and ceremony.

---

## 💡 What This Story Teaches

This story reminds us:

> Adversity can be overcome with faith and determination.  
> From one journey, many families and communities can grow.  
> We must remember and celebrate the journeys of our ancestors.
