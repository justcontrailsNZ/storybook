# ✨🌟 The Legend of Matariki 🌟✨

In winter, a cluster of stars rises in the eastern sky before dawn. The Māori name for these stars is **Matariki**. It marks the Māori New Year, a time for remembering those who have passed, celebrating the harvest, and planning for the year ahead.

But there is a story of how Matariki came to be.

---

## 👁️ The Eyes of the Wind

Long ago, the god **Tāwhirimātea**, the lord of winds and storms, was angry. His parents, Ranginui and Papatūānuku, had been separated. His brothers had helped push them apart, and Tāwhirimātea was furious.

He tore out his own eyes and threw them into the sky. They became the stars of Matariki—a mother star surrounded by her six daughters.

Some say the stars are a reminder of Tāwhirimātea’s rage. Others say they are a symbol of hope.

---

## 🌾 A Time of Renewal

When Matariki appears, the cold winter is not yet over. But the stars bring promise. People gather to remember ancestors, share stories, and plant new crops. They look at the stars and reflect on the year that has passed.

In some traditions, if the stars are bright and clear, it means the coming year will be warm and plentiful. If they are hazy, the harvest may be hard.

---

## 💡 What Matariki Teaches

Matariki is a time to:

> Remember those who have died and celebrate those who live.  
> Share food and stories with family and friends.  
> Look to the stars and think about the future.
