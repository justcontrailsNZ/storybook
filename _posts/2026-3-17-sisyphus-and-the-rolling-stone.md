title: "Sisyphus and the Rolling Stone"
---


Long ago, there was a king named Sisyphus. He was clever—sometimes too clever. He liked tricking people so he could get what he wanted.

At first, Sisyphus’ tricks seemed funny to him. He would grin and think, “I’m smarter than everyone.”

But his tricks often hurt others. People began to lose trust in him.

A king who wouldn’t stop
Sisyphus made promises he didn’t keep. He bent rules whenever it suited him. If someone warned him, he shrugged and said, “It’s fine.”

In Greek myths, choices matter, and sooner or later Sisyphus had to face the results of how he acted.

After Sisyphus died, the gods decided to give him a punishment that matched his behaviour.

It would not be a quick punishment. It would be a lesson that lasted a long time.

The endless task
Sisyphus found himself in a strange, empty place with a steep hill. At the bottom of the hill was a giant stone.

A voice told him, “Your task is to push this stone to the top.”

Sisyphus put his shoulder against the stone and pushed.

The stone was heavy. It scraped along the ground. Sisyphus’ legs shook, but he kept going.

Step by step, he pushed it up the hill.

At last he reached the top.

Sisyphus smiled with relief. “Done,” he panted.

But then the stone began to roll.

It rolled faster and faster down the other side—back to the bottom.

Sisyphus stared, shocked.

“No,” he whispered.

The voice said, “Now push it up again.”

So Sisyphus pushed.

And again, just as he reached the top, it rolled back down.

Over and over, the same job, with no finish line.

What Sisyphus learned
At first, Sisyphus felt angry. “This is unfair!” he shouted.

But after a long time, Sisyphus began to understand the lesson. His whole life, he had tried to get ahead using tricks. He wanted easy wins and quick results.

Now he had a task that could not be solved by tricking anyone.

It could only be faced with patience.

Some people say this myth teaches: if you keep making dishonest choices, you can end up stuck, doing the same hard work again and again.

It also teaches that trust is precious. Once it is broken, it can be difficult to repair.

Why people still tell it
Today, when someone says a job feels like “pushing a boulder uphill,” they mean it feels never-ending.

The story of Sisyphus reminds us to make good choices early—because choices can shape what happens next.

Quick glossary: trust (believing someone will do the right thing), punishment (a consequence), task (a job you must do), endless (never finishing)
