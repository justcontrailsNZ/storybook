title: "Hermes and Argus"
category: Greek

Long ago, the gods sometimes argued and caused trouble for each other. One powerful goddess, Hera, was especially watchful. She wanted things done her way, and she did not like being tricked.

One day, Hera decided she needed a guard—someone who could watch without blinking, day and night.

She chose Argus, a giant with a hundred eyes.

Some of Argus’ eyes could sleep, but not all at once. That meant Argus was always watching.

Hera’s careful guard
Hera gave Argus an important job: protect a special animal she wanted to keep safe. Argus took the job seriously.

He stood near the animal all day. He watched the hills. He watched the trees. He watched the sky.

No one could sneak past him.

People whispered, “You can’t fool Argus. He sees everything.”

But another god, Hermes, was known for being clever and quick. Hermes was a messenger, but he was also good at solving tricky problems.

Hermes was asked to rescue the animal from Argus’ guard.

Hermes looked toward Argus in the distance and said, “All right. But I won’t win by strength. I’ll need a plan.”

Hermes approaches
Hermes put on a simple cloak and walked toward Argus like an ordinary traveller.

Argus’ eyes narrowed. “Stop,” he said. “Who are you?”

Hermes smiled politely. “Just a shepherd,” he said. “I’m looking for a place to rest.”

Argus did not move. “You can rest somewhere else.”

Hermes sat down anyway, not too close, not too far. He began to talk in a calm, boring voice.

He told long stories about sheep. He described the same hills again and again. He counted imaginary goats. He repeated himself on purpose.

Argus stared, suspicious at first. But Hermes’ voice was steady and slow, like a lullaby.

Some of Argus’ eyes began to droop.

Hermes noticed and continued.

Music and sleep
Then Hermes did something smarter. He took out a small instrument (in some versions, a flute) and began to play gentle music.

The notes floated through the air, soft as wind through grass.

Argus tried to stay alert. “I’m not tired,” he muttered.

But Hermes’ music didn’t rush. It didn’t shout. It simply continued, calm and quiet.

More of Argus’ eyes closed.

Argus fought it. He blinked and blinked, but his eyelids grew heavy.

At last, for the first time ever, all of Argus’ eyes fell asleep at the same time.

Hermes whispered, “Now.”

The rescue
Hermes quickly rescued the guarded animal and led it away to safety. He didn’t waste time. He knew Hera would notice soon.

When Argus woke, it was too late. The animal was gone, and Hermes had disappeared.

Argus was ashamed. “How did I fall asleep?” he wondered.

Hera’s memory
Hera was furious at first, but she also respected Argus for being a loyal guard. In the myth, Hera wanted to remember Argus and his many eyes.

So she placed a symbol of Argus’ eyes onto the feathers of a beautiful bird: the peacock.

That is why, in Greek stories, peacocks are linked to Hera, and why their feathers look like they have many “eyes.”

What this myth teaches
This myth shows that:

Cleverness can solve problems that strength cannot.
Being watchful is important, but everyone can get tired.
Some stories explain patterns in nature, like peacock feathers.
Quick glossary (word help)

suspicious: feeling unsure or worried about something
lullaby: a soft song that helps someone fall asleep
symbol: something that stands for an idea
