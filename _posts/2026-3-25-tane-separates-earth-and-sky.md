# 🌏🌌 Tāne Separates Earth and Sky 🌌🌏

Long ago, **Ranginui** (the Sky Father) and **Papatūānuku** (the Earth Mother) were locked in a tight embrace. Their children—the gods—lived in the darkness between them, crushed and unable to move.

The children grew tired of the cramped darkness. They wanted light and space to breathe.

“We must separate our parents,” said Tūmatauenga, the god of war. “I will kill them.”

“No,” said Tāne, the god of forests and birds. “We can push them apart without violence.”

---

## 💪 The Great Effort

One by one, the gods tried to push Ranginui and Papatūānuku apart. Rongo, the god of cultivated food, tried. Then Tangaroa, the god of the sea, tried. But none could budge them.

Finally, Tāne took a deep breath. He placed his shoulders against his mother, Papatūānuku, and his feet against his father, Ranginui.

He pushed with all his might.

The earth groaned. The sky trembled.

Slowly, inch by inch, Ranginui rose upward. Light poured into the space between them for the first time.

---

## 🌳 The Forests Grow

When Tāne straightened his legs, the sky lifted high above. Papatūānuku lay beneath, now bathed in light.

Tāne looked around. The world was empty—only rocks and dirt. He wanted to clothe his mother in beauty.

He took his children, the trees and birds, and placed them on Papatūānuku. Forests grew. Birds filled the air. Flowers bloomed.

Tāne’s brother, Tāwhirimātea, the god of storms, was furious that his parents had been separated. He attacked his brothers with winds and rain. But Tāne stood firm, and the world remained divided.

---

## 💡 A New World

From that day, Ranginui watches over Papatūānuku from above. His tears become rain, and his sighs become the wind. Papatūānuku provides food, shelter, and a home for all living things.

This story teaches:

> Sometimes difficult changes are needed for new life to grow.  
> Strength can come from determination, not violence.  
> We owe our world to the courage of our ancestors.
